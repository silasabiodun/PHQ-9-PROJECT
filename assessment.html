History of PHQ-9

The PHQ-9 is one module of the Patient Health Questionnaire and has nine questions that are simple and short. These questions were designed to help healthcare professionals identify signs or symptoms of depression

The scientists namely, Dr. Robert L. Spitzer, Dr. Janet B.W. Williams, and Dr. Kurt Kroenke developed PHP first in the mid-1990s then PHP-Q in 1999. It was only in 2001 did PHQ-9 get introduced into the primary health settings.

This questionnaire comprises of the same criteria used in the diagnosis of depression under the current "Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders" (DSM-5) and include:

1. Feeling depressed or irritable.
2. Loss of interest or loss of pleasure in doing things.
3. Overeating or loss of appetite.
4. Sleep issues (either sleeping too much or trouble falling or staying asleep)
5. Moving or speaking slowly, or the opposite, speaking quickly or being fidgety
6. Feeling fatigued or lacking energy
7. Feeling like a failure, or that you have disappointed your family or yourself
8. Difficulty concentrating, such as when watching TV or reading a news app
9. Thinking you'd be better off dead or wishing you could hurt yourself somehow

About the uses of PHQ-9, the PHQ-9 may be a component of a full psychiatric screening process that evaluates all areas of mental health. The full three-page PHQ assessment is used by some practitioners to assess whether certain mental health issues may be causing or increasing a patient's physical health concerns.

In addition to looking at depression, the complete PHQ asks about alcohol use, anxiety, and eating behaviors.﻿﻿ Patients' answers can help identify potential issues in these areas.
